What is it that we were trying to solve?

It began with a familiar scene every semester - friends rushing to vacate dorms with nowhere to store their belongings. Traditional storage was expensive, off-campus, and inconvenient, forcing students to overpay, share risky spaces, or leave things behind. IDEATION

Figuring out how to bring structure to chaos

Before diving into design, our team came together for a focused ideation sprint. Using insights from user research and early validation, we explored how affordability, safety, and trust could coexist in a peer-to-peer storage model.

The goal was simple - to turn research findings into actionable design directions that could define the MVP. Each idea was evaluated for user impact, technical feasibility, and alignment with our core problem: making storage simpler, safer, and more transparent for students.




IDEATION

What do we build first?

The team had a lot of great ideas - from dashboards to native apps, and even chatbots. They were asking for ChatGPT-style functionality before ChatGPT even existed!

But with a lean team and a tight launch window, we needed to focus. Working closely with the founders, I helped prioritize features that would let us launch quickly, learn fast, and validate user trust - without overbuilding.

The goal wasn‚Äôt to design the most feature-rich app, but to build something affordable, trustworthy, and testable - a version we could put in users‚Äô hands fast enough to gather real feedback. To ensure this, I used a hybrid approach combining the MoSCoW framework and a Value‚ÄìEffort matrix to define what was essential for launch versus what could wait.




Scoping the MVP using MoSCoW

Evaluating Impact using a Value-Effort Matrix


After defining the MVP scope, I quickly mapped ideas on a Value-Effort Matrix to visualize trade-offs. This clarified which features delivered the most user value for the least build complexity.

Core features like the listings map and booking system ranked high on value and low on effort, becoming our MVP foundation. Advanced ideas like AI-based estimation, while exciting, fell into high-effort quadrants and were deferred for later phases.

So at the end of it all, we designed IA and wireframes around our initial and primary target audience: Student Renters

Introducing BoxMate

The design centered around two principles - trust and affordability.

Every interaction, layout, and color choice reinforced reliability without looking corporate or expensive. Soft lavender tones, clean typography, and open spacing created a calm, student-friendly aesthetic that felt accessible and approachable.

In just eight weeks, we launched BoxMate‚Äôs first responsive web platform - a simple, trustworthy, and affordable storage solution for students looking to bridge their lease gaps.

V1 Launch - Friction and Learnings

The first release was everything we wanted it to be as a team - simple, secure, and ready to earn trust. But once live, the data told a humbling story.

To protect hosts‚Äô privacy, we decided to hide exact addresses and location details until a renter logged in. The intent was safety - but in practice, it added friction. Users wanted transparency before commitment. Four weeks after launch, 85% of bookings were still happening manually, and analytics showed where the trust broke down.

design

50+ Surveys Later - Validating the metrics before fixing

After reviewing the numbers low conversion rates and a steep drop-off, we wanted to validate our findings with users.
We reached out to early renters, students who had started but not completed bookings, and a few potential renters to understand what would make them leave.

Through 50+ structured surveys, we focused on uncovering the possible friction points.

design

V2 Launch - Show, Don‚Äôt Gatekeep

Our post-launch findings made one thing clear: users trusted our brand but hesitated to commit without visibility. They wanted to see enough to believe enough.

For Version 2, we redesigned the renter experience to build trust through openness while still protecting host privacy. The goal was to preserve security, but remove unnecessary friction - helping users explore freely before taking action.

design

Designing for everyone

We realized early on that for many students, trust also meant accessibility.

Our product was meant to serve a wide range of renters - including first-time users, out-of-state students, and those accessing the platform on low-end devices or in shared networks.
To make BoxMate genuinely inclusive, we focused on designing for:

>
01

Students browsing on low-end or shared devices

Many renters accessed the site through borrowed laptops or older phones, so we optimized layouts for smaller screens and slower connections.

02

Users with visual strain or limited attention span

High-contrast color palette (WCAG AA compliant), accessible typography, and clear CTAs made key information legible even under poor lighting or mobile glare.

03

First-time platform users seeking reassurance

For those hesitant about online payments or data sharing, we simplified form fields, used familiar iconography, and clarified trust badges (verified hosts, Stripe checkout, privacy messages).

Reflection

Project Takeaways

Looking back, BoxMate taught me that even the most well-intentioned design decisions can backfire if they‚Äôre not validated with users early enough.
Our instinct to ‚Äúguard‚Äù information came from a good place - protecting privacy and building trust - but it unintentionally created barriers.

Through this process, I learned three key things:

01

Trust is earned through transparency, not restriction.

Real trust came when we allowed users to explore freely, not when we tried to protect them behind logins or gated flows.

02

Affordability extends beyond pricing.

Making a product affordable also means making it frictionless - low effort, low confusion, and quick to act on.

03

Data tells the story design can‚Äôt.

Post-launch analytics and surveys were more revealing than any internal assumption. They shaped our decisions faster and more objectively than discussion ever could.

If I were to revisit BoxMate today, I‚Äôd integrate usability testing earlier in the design cycle - before committing to a flow.
