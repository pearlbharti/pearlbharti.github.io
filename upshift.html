Introduction

Background

This project was completed as part of the User Experience Design (LIS 612) course in the Information Science program at the University of Wisconsin–Madison. As part of the assignment, we could choose any theme from UW–Madison’s RISE Initiative, and I chose RISE-EARTH, focusing on sustainability and environmental impact. I enjoyed exploring how UX design can contribute to a more sustainable future.




PROBLEM

The Alarming Environmental Cost of Fast Fashion

Global warming is accelerating due to human activities, with the fashion industry contributing around 10% of global carbon emissions —more than international flights and maritime shipping combined . Garment waste is a major factor, with over 92 million tons generated annually, much of it ending up in landfills. While individuals can help reduce their environmental footprint by embracing sustainable fashion—such as buying secondhand, choosing durable items, and supporting eco-friendly brands—barriers like stigma, convenience, and limited awareness prevent many from making these changes.

SOLUTION

Turning thrifting into a seamless, trendy, and impactful choice

Upshift's sustainable mission inspired me to reimagine their app after it became my go-to for affordable warmth in Wisconsin's cold. Post learning about Upshift, Madison-WI's only swap shop.







‍
SOLUTION #1

Smart & Hassle-Free Thrifting

Items are sorted by style, trend, and size for easy browsing. Users can buy personalized thrifted gift bundles. Each item has clear descriptions, condition details, and sustainability impact.

SOLUTION #2

Making Secondhand Shopping More Appealing

The app offers outfit ideas to style thrifted clothes. Unique pieces come with background stories. A smooth shopping experience makes thrifting easy and fun.

SOLUTION #3

Encouraging Sustainable Choices with Rewards

Gaia, the in-app mascot, teaches users about sustainable fashion. Users earn points and discounts for eco-friendly shopping.

Although, desk research provided macro-level insights on fast fashion’s impact and barriers to sustainable fashion, highlighting gaps in consumer behavior. I conducted Secondary research to validate these findings, uncovering psychological barriers, peer influence, and the need for trust and convenience in second-hand fashion adoption.

RESEARCH

Affinity Mapping

I conducted interviews and surveys with my peers at UW Madison to explore the decision-making in secondhand shopping, sustainability goals, spending habits, and purchase motivations. I then distilled the data along with the rest to inform brainstorming for affinity mapping


RESEARCH

What I concluded...

After multiple rounds of research and market analysis, key barriers to secondhand shopping—convenience, stigma, and engagement—became clear.


#1
Convenience Drives Adoption
Users hesitate to thrift due to overwhelming inventory and lack of organization. A streamlined browsing experience with curated selections and smart filters can improve engagement.
#2
Stigma Still Exists
Despite sustainability awareness, many associate secondhand shopping with lower quality. Providing product history, styling inspiration, and a guided experience (like Gaia) can reshape perceptions
#3
Gamification Boosts Engagement
Rewarding users with impact tracking and incentives can reinforce sustainable habits, making thrifting feel both impactful and enjoyable.

Ideation

A Moodboard for Playful & Conscious Sustainability

I focused on warm, earthy tones and organic textures to create a sense of nostalgia, comfort, and authenticity—key emotions that make secondhand shopping feel like an experience rather than a transaction.

The mix of modern typography, and vintage-inspired visuals reinforces the idea that sustainability can be stylish, fun, and deeply personal.



The Mascot

Inspired by Duolingo’s engaging mascot, I created Gaia in Figma to make sustainability feel interactive and fun. Named after the Greek goddess of Earth, she serves as a friendly guide, reinforcing eco-conscious choices.




Ideation

Task Flows

With my visual direction in place, I mapped out 4 taskflows to begin designing a seamless and engaging user journey. Every step designed with clarity and fun in mind—guided by Gaia, of course!







Ideation

Paper Prototyping

I created a quick paper prototype to gather early feedback from three participants, and their experiences brought several key issues to light. Rachel

  • Styling Tips Visibility: Rachel initially missed the styling tips feature, underscoring the need for more prominent placement. “I didn’t notice the styling tips until much later—it needs to stand out more.”
  • Loyalty Points Labeling: Blake had trouble locating loyalty points, confused by the term "Sustainability Points." “I wasn’t sure if Sustainability Points were the same as loyalty points.”
  • Profile Placement: Valentine’s journey went smoothly but recommended moving the profile section to a more noticeable area for easy access.




Ideation

Testing and Design Iterations

To bring about more completeness to my designs yet leaving scope for change and adjustments given the incipient stage, I roughly sketched screen designs. I very visibly changed the layout of the screen with the socials at the bottom, the icon and the navbar on top.

Design

Design System

The design system was the blueprint for my consistency. It defined core elements like color, typography, and layout, ensuring everything I created is cohesive and accessible. Adding auto layout and reusable components really paid off when I revisited the design a year later, making the process more efficient and flexible. It also ensured my designs work seamlessly, reflecting my commitment to quality.

Design

Final Prototypes

The culmination of my design journey lies in the latest mockups, born from a series of refinements initiated from a dull, monochromatic website. Now, it boasts a vibrant pastel color scheme, reflecting the playful essence of Upshift Swap Shop. Additionally, various features have been integrated to enhance online engagement, including personalized gift options and a loyalty points system, among others.

Reflection

What I Learnt and a little about the timeline!

It’s been a year since the initial kickoff of the project in Jan 2024. After a three-month sprint that produced the first case study, feedback from a mentor and fellow designers led me to revisit the project in January 2025. In this refreshed phase, I:

  • Revisited my research and deepened my understanding by reading new academic papers, which provided fresh insights and helped shape a more informed approach.
  • Refined visual explorations and added new features
  • Shifted towards a more sustainability focused direction for the app

Beyond refining the project’s direction, I discovered the immense value of iterative design and continuous feedback. Engaging with academic literature not only enriched my insights but also reinforced the importance of clear communication, intuitive labeling, and engaging features—all crucial elements in creating a more impactful and sustainable product.

reflection

What's next?

Coming back to this project really helped me grow as a designer. I got a deeper sense of how design and product strategy work together, and each round of iteration has taught me something new about the user’s needs. I'm looking forward to how the next challenges in this space will push my design thinking even further.

  • Refine and Implement Impact Metrics: Add metrics that showcase the impact of each purchase, such as the amount of water saved or trees preserved, to help users see the tangible results of their actions.
  • Make the platform more social: Enhance social features by allowing users to share their sustainability achievements on social media, view collective impact, and participate in challenges or community boards.
  • Focus on User Retention: Analyze retention patterns and address friction points. Implement features like gamification and progress tracking to motivate users to complete lessons and stay engaged.
