background

Keepers of Wisconsin's Natural Beauty

The Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources (WDNR) manages the state’s natural resources and provides information on outdoor activities like hunting, fishing, and hiking. The WDNR website is a vital resource for residents, tourists, and nature enthusiasts seeking park details, trail maps, permits, and educational content. However, its complex design creates usability issues, making it difficult for users to efficiently find park information, obtain permits, or understand regulations.

RESEARCH GOAL

Evaluating usability and efficiency in accessing key park and permit information

Research goal of the usability study conducted specifically focused on the ease with which users can find essential information about state parks, such as hours of operation, entrance fees, facilities, and trail information. With the aim to evaluate the efficiency, effectiveness, and user satisfaction of the site when performing common tasks related to park visits. Specifically, to identify usability issues that hinder the effective completion of key tasks such as applying for licenses, finding information about environmental programs, and navigating the homepage.

RESEARCH QUESTIONS

The primary goal of this usability study was to understand how effectively users could navigate the website to find essential information about state parks.


Can users easily locate detailed information about specific state parks, such as hours of operation, entrance fees, and available amenities?
This question was designed to evaluate the ease with which users could locate basic but essential information for park visitors, including operational hours, fees, and park facilities (e.g., restrooms, picnic areas).
Can users successfully navigate to the camping and trails sections to find relevant details about campsite amenities and trail information?
This question sought to explore whether users could easily access information regarding camping amenities (e.g., electrical hookups, water availability) and trail details (e.g., trail length, difficulty, and conditions).
How satisfied are users with the overall navigation and information architecture of the website?
This question aimed to assess user satisfaction regarding the general navigation flow and the ease with which they could find desired information across the website.
PARTICIPANT RECrUITMENT

Recruitment method

My recruitment plan for the usability study aimed to engage a diverse group of participants, including both regular and occasional users of the website. To ensure varied perspectives, I targeted individuals with different levels of tech proficiency and demographic backgrounds.

Recruitment was carried out through social media platforms, community groups, and snowball sampling.
  • Social Media: A post about the usability study was shared in local Wisconsin Facebook groups and Reddit forums (r/UWMadison) related to Wisconsin, which helped reach people with an interest in the Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources (DNR).
  • Snowball Sampling: After recruiting the initial participants, we asked them to recommend others who might be interested, which broadened the participant pool to include a variety of user experiences.

To streamline participant selection, I utilized a brief screening survey to confirm eligibility. This plan was designed to gather a well-rounded yet fitting perspective on the website’s usability.

PARTICIPANT RECrUITMENT

Developing Screener Survey

To refine my participant pool, I designed an anonymous screener survey using Google Forms. By withholding the study's true topic, I ensured unbiased, truthful responses for a reliable selection process. Further, I made use of targeted questions to ensure that I comply with my selection criteria. This helped ensure participants matched the typical user base of the Wisconsin DNR website.


  1. Interest in exploring natural attractions like parks and trails.

Usability Study

The D-Day

After weeks and weeks research, task iterations, and pilot studies. It was finally the much awaited week of conducting the final usability tests on the selected participants.This moderated remote usability study evaluated the Wisconsin DNR website's ease of use, where participants completed tasks under my guidance (moderator) to simulate an authentic browsing experience. Insights were gathered in real-time from the in-test, pre-test and post-test questions along with the standardized scores collected to identify usability challenges and suggest design changes to improve the overall user experience.

Usability Study

Tasks

The participants were given a series of realistic tasks that represented typical actions a visitor to the Wisconsin DNR website would perform. These tasks were designed to test the ease of navigation, information retrieval, and satisfaction. Participants were asked to complete the given 4 tasks.

  • Task 1: Finding Operating Hours
    • Goal: Find the hours of operation for Devil’s Lake State Park.
  • Task 2: Finding Entrance Fees
    • Goal: Find the entrance fees for Devil’s Lake State Park.
  • Task 3: Locating Facilities (e.g., Restrooms, Picnic Areas)
    • Goal: Identify at least two facilities available at Devil’s Lake State Park, such as restrooms and picnic areas.
  • Task 4: Locating Trail Information
    • Goal: Find detailed information on the East Bluff Trail, including its length, difficulty, and safety guidelines.
Usability Study

Study setup and logistics

The usability study was conducted remotely, with participants completing tasks on the Wisconsin DNR website from their own devices. Sessions, lasting 30-45 minutes, were guided by me through video conferencing, where participants were asked to do specific tasks. The sessions were video-recorded via zoom with participants' consent, capturing their feedback and think-aloud responses, and supplemented by detailed notes. Post the pilot test, a few adjustments were made during the process to prevent techincal difficulties and an iteration on the clarity of tasks.

USABILITY STUDY

In-Test Questions

During the tasks, participants were prompted to think aloud and provide insight into their experience as they interacted with the website.

  • “What do you notice first on this page?”
  • “Can you describe what you’re looking for as you complete this task?”
  • “Is there anything on this page that you find unclear or difficult to navigate?”
  • “How does the layout or content of this page influence your next steps?”

These questions were designed to encourage participants to articulate their thoughts, helping to identify usability issues as they performed the tasks.

USABILITY STUDY

Post-Test Questions

After completing the tasks, participants answered several standardized questions designed to assess the overall user experience. These included:

  1. Signal Ease Question (SEQ)
    • The SEQ helps measure the subjective difficulty of performing a specific task on the website. Participants were asked to rate how easy or difficult they found each task (e.g., “How easy was it to find the hours of operation?”). The rating scale typically ranges from 1 (very difficult) to 7 (very easy).
  2. Net Promoter Score (NPS)
    • The NPS measures overall user satisfaction and willingness to recommend the website. Participants were asked: “On a scale of 0-10, how likely are you to recommend the Wisconsin DNR website to a friend or colleague?”
  3. Post-Task Feedback
    • After each task, participants were asked to provide verbal feedback about their experience. They were encouraged to think aloud during the process, which helped capture their thought process and any frustrations or difficulties encountered. Specific questions included:
      • “Was there anything particularly frustrating or confusing while completing this task?”
      • “How confident are you that you found the correct information?”

Methods to measure task outcomes


SEQ, NPS, and Time on Task were chosen over other metrics for their ability to balance quantitative and qualitative insights. SEQ directly measures task difficulty , offering granular feedback per task, while NPS captures overall satisfaction and user loyalty. Time on Task provides objective data on efficiency, making these metrics complementary and efficient for understanding usability without overwhelming participants or complicating the analysis.

RESULTS

Navigation hurdles and information access issues

The WDNR website suffers from confusing navigation, inconsistent labeling, and a cluttered design, making it difficult for users to find key information like permits and licenses. Key tasks require excessive clicks and scrolling, leading to a frustrating user experience, especially for first-time visitors.
The study uncovered multiple recurring usability challenges, outlined below with their severity levels, frequency levels, participant feedback, and supporting examples.



Issue 1: Difficulty Locating Park Information

High Severity High Frequency

Participants struggled to find specific information about state parks, such as fees, amenities, and trail maps. The navigation menus were not intuitive, and key information was buried under multiple layers of links.





Metrics

  • Time on Task - 3 minutes 32 seconds (intended completion time was <. 2 minutes).
  • SEQ - 1 participants rated it 3/7 (diffficult), 2 participants rated it 5/7 (moderately easy).
  • NPS - 1 participants gave a rating of 3/10 (dissatisfied), 2 participant gave a rating of 6/10 (neutral).

Design Implications

  1. Revise Navigation: Simplify navigation by grouping related content (e.g., “Parks & Trails,” “Fees & Permits”) and use a side or mega menu for quick access.
  2. Dedicated Parks Section: Create a distinct “Parks & Trails” section with direct links to essential information (fees, maps, amenities).
  3. Add a filter for parks only, forests only, trails only and others.

Issue 2: Overwhelming Homepage Design

Moderate Severity Moderate Frequency

The homepage featured too many visual elements competing for the user's attention, such as sliders, images, and numerous links. Users found it hard to prioritize or locate critical information.





Metrics

  • Average Time on Task - 2 minutes (participants struggled to locate key areas quickly).
  • SEQ - 2 participants rated it 2/7 (very difficult),1 participant rated it 4/7 (moderate).
  • NPS - 2 participants gave a rating of 3/10 (dissatisfied), 1 participant gave a rating of 5/10 (neutral).

Design Implications

  1. Streamline Homepage: Reduce visual clutter by focusing on primary calls to action (e.g., park search, permit application).

Issue 3: Lack of User Guidance for First-Time Visitors

Moderate Severity Low Frequency

The site lacked a “Getting Started” section or any user guidance for those new to the site. First-time visitors found it challenging to understand where to go for basic information about DNR services.





Metrics

  • 1 participant rated it 4/7 (moderate), 2 participants rated it 5/7 (easy).
  • NPS: 2 participants gave a rating of 5/10 (neutral), 1 participant gave a rating of 6/10 (satisfied).

Design Implications

  1. “Getting Started” Section: Add a dedicated "Getting Started" section to guide new users through common tasks.
  2. Onboarding Tour: Implement an optional walkthrough for first-time visitors to familiarize them with key features.
  3. Help/FAQ Section: Create a visible Help or FAQ section for quick answers to common questions.

Reflection

Future Work

As my research and design process highlighted critical areas for improvement on the WDNR's website, I envision several next steps to refine and elevate the user experience as we intend to implement the design changes.

  • Balancing Structure and Flexibility - A well-structured approach with room for flexibility, especially in think-aloud sessions, revealed deeper insights into user behavior.
  • Comprehensive Data - Combining both quantitative and qualitative data (success rate, SEQ, NPS) provided a complete picture of user experience.
  • Actionable Feedback - Post-task feedback highlighted areas for improvement that were not visible during the tasks, reinforcing the importance of user input in the process.
