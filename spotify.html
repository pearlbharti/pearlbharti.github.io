Challenge

Spotify’s music discovery felt impersonal and unclear, so we set out to understand why users were losing trust in recommendations, and how UX design could help.

Opportunity

We saw an opportunity to uncover what was breaking Spotify’s discovery experience- and use research-driven insights to guide more personalized, intuitive listening.






OVERVIEW

What This Project Was About

I led a UX research sprint to explore pain points in Spotify’s recommendation and discovery experience. Over 8 weeks, our two-person team conducted interviews and behavioral observations to surface user frustrations—then synthesized findings into actionable design suggestions. The goal: make Spotify’s discovery feel more transparent, intuitive, and personal.

Problem

Too Much Guesswork, Not Enough Me

Spotify’s once-magical recommendations now feel like a mismatch for many users. From Discover Weekly that plays ambient piano tracks no one asked for, to Enhance buttons adding songs from unknown genres, users are losing trust in the system that’s supposed to know them best.

When the Algorithm Stops Listening

🎧 Then (Spotify at Its Best)

  • Discover Weekly nailed my vibe.
  • I felt like the algorithm knew me
  • Music was fresh, exciting, on point
  • I trusted the Enhance button

😕 Now (User Frustration)

  • Why am I getting sleep music at 2PM?
  • My recs haven’t changed in weeks.
  • It’s the same 5 artists, over and ever
  • I avoid Enhance—it ruins the playlist

As the gap between user expectations and Spotify’s output grew, so did frustration—and a clear opportunity for design-led research.

Objectives and Scope

Project Rationale

Early in our exploration, we noticed a pattern : users were voicing similar concerns about Spotify’s recommendation features feeling off, stale, or hard to trust. Discovery- a defining part of Spotify’s experience—was becoming a point of friction.


Instead of focusing on algorithmic changes, we aimed at exploring how UX design can bridge the gap between Spotify’s intelligence and its interface. There was a clear opportunity to enhance how users engage with personalization—through more transparency, feedback, and control- so the experience feels more aligned with who they are now, not just who they were.

Plan and Conduct Research

Target Population

To understand where personalization succeeds- and where it breaks- we spoke to people who fit these profiles:

  • Current Spotify users (Free & Premium)
  • Switchers who left Spotify for another service
  • Competing platform users (e.g., Apple Music, YouTube Music)
  • Multi-platform users who regularly toggle between apps
  • Accessibility-focused users using screen readers or assistive tools

User Persona

Tara

Age: 26

Status: Free User

Listening Time: 3–4 hrs/day


  • "I love discovering new music... until Spotify starts getting weird and recommends sea shanties."

Background

Tara is a grad student juggling coursework, side projects, and gym sessions. Music is her main escape, often played in the background during deep work. She loves variety, but doesn’t have time to micromanage her playlists or teach the algorithm how to do its job.

Goals & Motivations

  • Quickly discover music that matches her mood
  • Build aesthetically perfect playlists with minimal effort.
  • Feel seen by the algorithm (emotionally, not literally).
  • Avoid feeling overwhelmed or “lost” in Spotify’s features.

Frustrations

  • Discover Weekly feels repetitive after a few months.
  • Enhance adds bizarre tracks she doesn’t like.
  • Doesn’t understand how recommendations are formed.
  • Can’t easily share or see what friends are listening to.

Core Needs

  • Low-effort ways to give Spotify feedback on recs.
  • Transparent explanations for “Why this song?”
  • Reliable, context-based playlist suggestions.
  • Surface social features when they’re relevant, not hidden in the void.
